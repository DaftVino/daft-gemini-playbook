# 086 — Manager One-on-One & Coaching Prep

- **Category:** Payroll, HR & Compliance
- **Surface:** Gemini in Docs
- **Risk:** Green/Yellow
- **Owner:** Field leadership
- **Last reviewed:** 2026-07-21
- **Required sources:** @Prior One-on-One Notes, @Current Action Plan, @Site KPI Report, @Open Commitments
- **Core blocks:** Evidence

## When to use

Before a recurring one-on-one, to make it a conversation about progress rather than a status report.

## Prompt

```text
Using @Prior One-on-One Notes, @Current Action Plan, @Site KPI Report, and @Open Commitments,
prepare for a one-on-one with [role] on [date].

Return:
(1) Commitments from last time: Commitment | Made by whom | Due | Status | Evidence. Include
    commitments **I** made, not only theirs — an unreturned commitment from the manager is the
    fastest way to make these meetings pointless.
(2) What has genuinely improved since last time, with evidence. Lead with this.
(3) Two or three topics worth the meeting, each with the specific evidence and an open question
(4) What they are likely to need from me — decisions, resources, escalations, air cover
(5) Anything from the KPI report that is more likely a system problem than a site problem
(6) One development topic, tied to something they have said they want

Keep it to one page. Do not turn this into a performance review, a list of everything off target,
or a script. Do not draw conclusions about their capability — prepare questions, not verdicts.
```

## Validation before use

- Section 1 is the point. If your own commitments are outstanding, address them first.
- Check whether apparent performance issues are system problems the manager cannot control.
- Notes from a one-on-one are records. Keep them factual and free of characterization.

## Example follow-up

`What is the one question that would tell me most about what is actually going on at this site?`
